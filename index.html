Three questions we need you to answer. The vendor recommendation is CTI. These are your calls.
Platform: Teams or Google Meet?
Your decision
CTI's BYOD system supports both platforms out of the box — anyone can plug in their laptop and run any meeting. The updated quote (May 12) now includes a Logitech Rally Bar + Tap IP Bundle, which supports both Teams and Google Meet. Once you decide, CTI configures the touchpad for your chosen platform — enabling one-tap meeting join without a personal laptop.
Option A Microsoft Teams — Ryder is already on Teams, which simplifies Ryder-Baton meeting join and matches your original AV requirements. Requires a Teams Room license (~$480/yr) and IT coordination with Ryder to provision the room account. Strongest alignment with the Ryder relationship.
Option B Google Meet — Baton's primary internal platform. No room license fee. Simpler to set up independently from Ryder IT. The tradeoff: Ryder participants join through Teams, not Google Meet, so there's no unified room controller experience for Ryder-facing meetings.
Context on the original scope
The original AV requirements said "Zoom and Google Meet are out of scope" — written for conference rooms with Ryder in mind. For the all-hands space, used mostly for Baton-internal events (TGIT, demos, company meetings), Google Meet is also a reasonable choice. This is your call to make with Ryder alignment as the key factor.
Display: 98" TV or projector?
Your decision
A 98" commercial TV is included in CTI's base quote at no additional cost. A projector is not in CTI's current scope (it was a Young Electric add-on at +$8,500) and would require electrical work and light removal, which likely needs landlord approval and is not portable.
Rec. 98" commercial TV — included in base quote. Works in ambient light without dimming. Fully portable if Baton moves. No electrical work, no landlord coordination, no bulb replacements. 98" is roughly 7 feet wide, ideal for rooms up to about 30 feet deep. Confirm room depth before closing this out.
Alt. Projector + screen — larger image for deeper rooms, more of an event feel. Downsides: requires dimming, electrical work, light removal, likely landlord approval, not portable, adds ~$8,500. Only worth considering if the room is deeper than 30-35 feet and back-row visibility is a real concern.
Gaming area: where does it go?
Your decision
The current all-hands space doubles as the gaming area. To reach 60-100 person capacity, the pool table and couches need to move. Three options:
Rec. Near the front door — cleanest separation. All-hands space becomes fully dedicated. Gaming becomes a culture signal at the entrance for visitors and guests. One check before committing: confirm the front area has roughly 14x17 feet of clearance including cue space.
Alt. Dining room — flexible if the front area is too small. Pool table and couches move to dining. TV on wheels follows. Dining room loses its primary function during gaming hours and would likely need a second TV.
Skip Keep in same space — least disruption, but limits capacity and creates an awkward vibe during all-hands. Not recommended if we're targeting 60-100 people.
